Swatter · living rule book

🤚 The rule book that learns your codebase — and never outgrows 4 KB

A small, self-maintaining file of review rules. Confirmed bugs teach it; noise decays out of it; a hard byte budget keeps its cost flat, review after review.

.swatter/rules.md ≤ 4 KB

The core idea

The whole book is pasted into every review — so its size is its cost

On every PR, Swatter drops the entire rule book verbatim into each finder's brief. That is what makes learned rules cost nothing extra to enforce — and exactly why the file can never be allowed to grow without bound.

Flat cost, not append-only growth

An unbounded log of every rule ever learned would make each review a little more expensive than the last. Swatter instead keeps a fixed ~4 KB budget and lets the book turn over — bad rules leave as good ones arrive. The bot gets sharper while the per-review token cost stays flat.

Anatomy of a rule

One rule is a generalized pattern a future diff could violate

Not “PR #42 forgot retry on line 88” (a one-off fact, never stored) but the class of mistake. Each entry carries its own evidence for the lifecycle to act on.

- id: r-2026-07-11-1 rule: Wrap external API calls in the shared withRetry helper origin: PR#42 2026-07-11 confidence: 0.90 path: api/client.go hits: 3 last_hit: 2026-07-10 misses: 1
rule

One actionable sentence — the general pattern, never the instance.

confidence

0–1 trust score. Rises slowly on hits, drops fast on misses.

path

File the rule is anchored to. If it leaves the repo, the rule expires.

hits / misses

Fired on a surviving finding vs. produced a rejected one.

last_hit

Recency — feeds the ~60-day decay used at eviction time.

origin

Where it was learned: the PR or branch and date.

The lifecycle — run once after each review

Learn → Dedup → Score → Compact

Four steps, in order, every review. Only the first adds; the other three are what keep the book honest and small.

1

Learn

Each CONFIRMED finding distills to at most one generalized candidate rule. No confirmed findings → nothing learned.

2

Dedup

Before inserting, a normalized-text match is a free reject; otherwise an LLM same-pattern judge catches paraphrase, generalization, and subset duplicates.

3

Score

Rule ids cited by a surviving finding gain a hit; ids behind a rejected candidate take a miss. Confidence moves accordingly.

4

Compact / expire

When over budget or every 20 reviews: drop dead-path rules, rank the rest by confidence × recency, and trim until the book fits 4 KB.

How it maintains the file size

Three forces hold the line at 4 KB

Size isn't managed by one rule — it's the net of what's kept out, what decays, and what gets evicted under budget.

1 · Dedup at the door

Paraphrases and subsets never enter. Exact-match-only guards silently accumulate near-duplicates — the semantic judge is what stops slow bloat.

2 · Scoring sorts wheat from noise

Hits nudge confidence up slowly; a single miss multiplies it by 0.7. Noise-making rules sink toward the eviction floor fastest.

3 · Compaction evicts under budget

Dead-path rules go immediately; the rest are ranked and the lowest are trimmed until the rendered book fits 4 KB again.

The scoring math

Trust is easy to lose, slow to earn

The asymmetry is deliberate: a rule has to prove itself many times to climb, but one burst of noise sends it toward eviction.

On a hit

c += (1 − c) · 0.1

Confidence eases toward 1 asymptotically — repeated confirmations raise it, but never in one jump. last_hit is stamped to today, resetting the recency clock.

On a miss

c · = 0.7

A rejected candidate cuts confidence by 30% at once. Two or three misses drop a rule below the eviction floor, where the next compaction removes it.

And recency decays the rest

At eviction time each rule's effective score is confidence × 2^(−days_since_last_hit / 60). A high-confidence rule that stopped firing months ago ranks below a fresh one — so the book keeps favoring rules that are earning their place now.
